I've Used AI Every Day for Two Years. Here's What Actually Scared Me.

Everyone's debating whether AI will take jobs. I was busy watching it reshape the way I think. That turned out to be the more unsettling story.

A
Arsalan AI Expert & Creative Technologist · Dubai
· May 2, 2026 · 7 min read
Illustration: Arsalan / AI

Let me start with something I haven't told many people.

About eight months into using AI tools daily for client work — generating images, writing scripts, building automations — I started noticing something strange. When I sat down to write something myself, without any AI, I felt a kind of friction I hadn't felt before. A hesitation. Like I was moving through water.

I brushed it off at first. Probably just fatigue. Long hours. Dubai summers do that to you.

But it kept happening. And slowly I started to understand what was going on. My brain had quietly outsourced parts of its process. Not my skills — those were still there. But the low-level cognitive scaffolding. The "what do I say next" muscle. The first-draft courage. I'd been using AI as a cognitive crutch so frequently that I'd let some of those neurons go a bit slack.

That was the moment I got genuinely unsettled. Not by what AI might do to the job market. By what it had already done to me, quietly, without asking permission.

The Job Question Is Real. But It's the Wrong Question.

I'm not dismissing the employment conversation — it's serious, and anyone who tells you AI won't displace jobs is either selling something or hasn't been paying attention. It already has. I've watched it happen in industries I work in.

But that discussion is happening at the macro level. Politicians, economists, think tanks. The conversation I want to have is more granular and, I think, more urgent: what happens to the individual person who uses AI intensively, every day, for years?

The machine didn't take my job. It slowly, politely, reshaped the inside of my head. And I let it because it felt like productivity.

This is what nobody is really talking about. The cognitive and psychological dimension of deep AI integration. We're all focused on the output — faster work, better results, more scale — and mostly ignoring what's happening to the inputs. To us.

Two Years of Daily Use: What I Actually Observed

I want to be precise here, because I think vague scaremongering is useless. So let me be specific about what I noticed, what I didn't, and what I genuinely don't know yet.

What got better (genuinely)

The volume and quality of my client output increased significantly. Things that used to take me three hours take forty minutes. My visual work — product photography, AI-generated campaigns — went from good to something I'm actually proud of. My ability to rapidly prototype ideas, test concepts, and iterate exploded. These are real gains and I don't want to undersell them.

I also got better at thinking in systems. Working with AI forces you to be precise about what you want. You have to articulate intent clearly or you get garbage back. That discipline, over time, made me a cleaner thinker in general. I'd call that an unexpected benefit.

Worth noting

A lot of the "AI is making us dumb" takes come from people who use it occasionally and poorly. The picture is more complicated for intensive daily users. There are real cognitive benefits that casual observers miss — and real costs that enthusiasts don't want to admit.

What got harder (and why it matters)

First-draft generation — the messy, unstructured, nobody-is-watching kind — got harder. Not impossible. Just slower and less natural than it used to be. I think the reason is simple: I'd been offloading that phase to AI so consistently that I'd practiced it less. Skills you don't use, you lose. Turns out this applies to creative cognition too.

Second, and this one took me a while to name: my tolerance for "good enough" output went down, but my definition of "good enough" started to drift toward what AI could produce easily. In some categories, that's fine. In others — especially work that's supposed to feel distinctly human — it's a subtle but real problem.

Third, and most interesting to me: my attention span inside individual tasks shortened. When you're used to generating ten variations in thirty seconds, sitting with one version for twenty minutes feels almost uncomfortable. I don't think this is entirely about AI — smartphones did a version of this years earlier — but the AI dimension is its own thing.

The Part That Surprised Me Most

I assumed that heavy AI use would make me lazy. It didn't. If anything, I work more than I did before. What it actually changed was my relationship to difficulty. Specifically: I became less comfortable with the productive frustration that often precedes a breakthrough.

The best creative work I've ever done — and probably you'd say the same about yours — came out of a period of being stuck. Of not knowing. Of trying things that didn't work. That discomfort is where something new gets made.

AI removes that discomfort very efficiently. Which sounds good on paper. But I've started to wonder what gets lost when we never have to sit in the problem long enough for something genuinely original to emerge.

Removing friction sounds like progress. But some friction is load-bearing. Take it out and the structure shifts in ways you don't notice until later.

The friction paradox — not all shortcuts lead to the same destination

So Is This a Warning? Sort Of.

I want to be careful not to be that guy who writes a think piece about technology being bad while using it constantly. I still use AI every day. I will tomorrow. The practical benefits are too significant to walk away from, and honestly, I genuinely enjoy working with these tools. There's something remarkable about having this kind of capability available.

But I've changed how I use it, and I think that matters.

I now deliberately do first drafts by hand — actual handwriting, when I can — before I bring any AI into a project. Not because it's more efficient, because it isn't. But because I want to protect that initial uninfluenced thinking. I want the idea to be mine before I throw it into the amplification machine.

I also started taking one full day a week where I don't use any AI tools at all. Just me, some notes, and whatever I can figure out on my own. It felt embarrassingly hard at first. Now it feels like maintenance. Like the cognitive equivalent of going for a run when you have a desk job.

What I Think Is Actually Coming

Here's my honest read on the next five years, based on what I've observed close up: the people who will be genuinely displaced are not the ones who use AI tools, and not even the ones who don't. The ones at real risk are those who use AI as a replacement for thinking rather than an extension of it.

There's a difference. And it's subtle enough that a lot of people are on the wrong side of it without knowing it.

Using AI to execute your ideas faster: great. Using AI because you don't want to have the ideas yourself: that's where things get complicated, for your work and, I'd argue, for something harder to name — your sense of authorship. Of agency. Of what it means to make something.

I don't think AI is taking over in the dramatic sci-fi sense. What I think is happening is quieter and, in some ways, more interesting. We are slowly negotiating, each of us individually, how much of our cognitive life to delegate. And most people are making that negotiation unconsciously, one convenience at a time, without ever deciding to decide.

That's what scared me when I noticed it in myself. Not the machine. The sleepwalking.

I'm not sure I have a clean conclusion to offer. I don't think this is a story with a moral. It's more an observation I wanted to write down before it got harder to remember what it felt like on the other side.

If you work with AI tools regularly, pay attention. Not to what you're producing. To what's happening inside your head while you produce it. That's where the interesting stuff is, and I don't see nearly enough people talking about it.

— Arsalan, Dubai, 2026

I've Used AI Every Day for Two Years. Here's What Actually Scared Me.

Everyone's debating whether AI will take our jobs. I was too busy watching it quietly rewire the way I actually think. That turned out to be the scarier story.

A
Arsalan AI Expert · Dubai
· May 2, 2026 · 8 min read
The quiet negotiation happening inside every person who uses AI daily Dubai, 2026

Let me start with something I haven't really said out loud before.

About eight months into using AI tools every single day for client work generating images, writing scripts, building automations, editing videos I started noticing something off. When I sat down to write something myself, without any AI, there was this weird friction. A hesitation I hadn't felt in years. Like starting a car that had been sitting idle too long.

I brushed it off at first. Long hours. Dubai summers. Whatever.

But it kept showing up. And slowly I started putting words to it. My brain had quietly outsourced parts of its own process. Not my skills those were still there. But the messy, first-step thinking. The willingness to start badly and fix it later. I'd been handing that phase to AI so consistently that I'd stopped practicing it myself.

That's the moment things got uncomfortable. Not because of what AI might do to the job market someday. Because of what it had already quietly done to me.

My actual setup in Dubai AI on one screen, me trying to figure out where the line was

The Job Question Is Real. But It's Not The Interesting One.

I'm not going to tell you AI won't displace jobs. It already has, in industries I work in directly. That conversation is real and serious and worth having.

But that's a macro story. Governments and economists are on it. The question I keep coming back to is smaller and more personal. What happens to you, as a person, when you use AI intensively every single day for years on end?

The machine didn't take my job. It quietly, politely, started reshaping the inside of my head. And I let it happen because it felt exactly like productivity.

Nobody writes about this. We're all focused on what AI produces the outputs, the time saved, the results and mostly ignoring what's happening to the person doing the producing.

Two Years In — What Actually Changed

Let me be specific, because vague takes are useless. Here's exactly what I noticed: what got better, what got harder, and what I genuinely still don't know.

What genuinely improved

Output went way up. Deliverables that used to take three hours take forty minutes. My visual work — product photography, AI-generated campaigns went from competent to something I'm actually proud of. And my ability to prototype ideas fast, test ten variations before lunch, iterate without the fear of "wasting" a whole afternoon on something that doesn't work? That's a real superpower now.

Something subtler also got better: I started articulating intent much more clearly. Working with AI forces precision. You can't be vague and expect good output. That discipline leaked into my non-AI work and made me a cleaner thinker overall. I didn't expect that one.

Before messy, slow, sometimes brilliant
After faster, cleaner, but something different
Worth saying

Most of the "AI is making us stupid" takes come from people who use it occasionally and badly. The picture is more complicated for daily intensive users. Real cognitive gains that skeptics miss, and real costs that enthusiasts won't admit. Both are true at the same time.

What quietly got harder

First-draft generation got harder. The messy, nobody-is-watching, just-start-somewhere kind of writing or thinking. Not impossible — just slower and less natural than before. I'd been offloading that phase so consistently that I'd stopped practicing it. Skills you don't use, you lose. Creative initiation is apparently not exempt from that rule.

Second: my tolerance for mediocre output dropped, but my definition of "good" started drifting toward what AI could produce easily. In some areas fine. In work that's supposed to feel human and personal, that drift is a quiet and real problem.

Third, most surprising of all: my attention span inside individual tasks shortened. When you can generate ten variations in thirty seconds, sitting with one thing for twenty minutes starts feeling weirdly uncomfortable. Like something is wrong. I don't think AI is entirely responsible for this but it absolutely adds its own layer.

Not a study just honest observations from two years of paying attention

The Thing That Surprised Me Most

I assumed heavy AI use would make me lazy. It didn't. I work more now than I did two years ago. What it actually changed was my relationship to difficulty itself.

The best work I've ever done probably the best work you've ever done too came out of a period of being genuinely stuck. Not knowing the answer. Trying things that didn't work. Sitting in discomfort long enough that something new emerged on the other side.

AI removes that discomfort really efficiently. And that sounds like a good thing. But I've been thinking about what gets lost when you never have to stay inside a problem long enough for something original to come out.

Removing friction sounds like progress. But some friction is structural. Take it out and the building shifts in ways you don't notice until later or until something falls.

Two paths to output knowing which one you need is the skill nobody talks about

What I Actually Changed

I'm not going to be the person who writes a whole essay about technology being bad while using it constantly. I still use AI every day. The benefits are too real. And honestly I love these tools — there's something genuinely remarkable about what's available right now.

But I made two specific changes after that realization hit.

First: I do first drafts by hand now. Pen on paper when I can manage it, or at minimum typing into a blank doc with everything closed. Not because it's efficient — it absolutely is not. Because I want the seed of the idea to be mine before I hand it to the amplification machine.

Second: one day a week, no AI tools. Just me and whatever I can figure out alone. The first few weeks this felt embarrassingly hard. Now it feels like maintenance. The cognitive equivalent of going for a run when you sit at a desk all day.

What I Think Is Actually Coming

Here's my honest read, based on two years of watching this up close in Dubai, working with clients across industries globally.

The people who will be genuinely displaced by AI are not the ones who use it, and not the ones who refuse to. The real risk is for people who use AI as a replacement for thinking rather than a tool for thinking faster.

There's a real difference. Subtle enough that a lot of people are on the wrong side of it without knowing it.

Using AI to execute your ideas faster that's leverage. Using AI because you don't want to have the ideas yourself I'm not sure that ends well.

I don't think AI is "taking over" in the sci-fi sense. What I think is happening is quieter and more interesting. We're each individually negotiating in real time how much of our cognitive life to delegate. And most people are making that negotiation unconsciously, one small convenience at a time, without ever actually deciding.

That's what scared me when I noticed it in myself. Not the machine. The fact that I was sleepwalking into a new version of how I think without ever choosing to.

Dubai, 2026 — somewhere between the future and figuring out what it costs

I don't have a clean conclusion. I don't think this is a story with a tidy moral. It's more just something I've been sitting with and wanted to write down before it got harder to remember what things felt like on the other side of it.

If you use AI tools regularly: pay attention. Not to what you're producing. To what's happening in your head while you produce it. The output is easy to measure. The input is where the actually interesting stuff lives, and I don't see nearly enough people talking about it.
